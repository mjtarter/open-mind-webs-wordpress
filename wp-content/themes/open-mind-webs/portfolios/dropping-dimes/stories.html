In 1962, Roger Brown was kicked out of college at the University of Dayton and banned from the NBA due to a gambling scandal in which he played no part. Roger, however wouldn't be denied his basketball destiny. After his college and pro playing days were seemingly over for good, Roger worked in a GM plant for six years in Dayton while playing for a local AAU team, but in 1967 the Indiana Pacers of the new ABA league came calling and gave him back the opportunity that was wrongfully taken from him. Life in the ABA

In many respects, the American Basketball Association has become lost in pro basketball history. The league lasted for 9 seasons and was a legitimate rival to the NBA in its heyday. It captured a large and extremely loyal following in its fans.

Part of the mystique of the ABA had to do with the fact that it was started from nothing and was very much of an outlaw league. It was much more exciting and wide open that the NBA was at that time, with its flashy red, white, and blue basketball, 3 point shot, run and gun style of play, and infamous game-day promotions, including the first female to “play” in a men’s pro basketball game. While the ABA players included blue chip college recruits, former NBA players, and players who were also drafted into the NBA but chose to play in the ABA, and although head-to-head games between ABA and NBA teams resulted in the ABA winning 79 match ups and the NBA winning 76, ABA players often played in smaller venues, and in front of smaller crowds, and rarely ever on television. Players who played all, or a significant part of their careers in the ABA included: Julius “Dr. J” Erving, George Gervin, Mel Daniels, Artis Gilmore, Rick Barry, Roger Brown, Dan Issel, Billy Cunningham, Connie Hawkins, David Thompson, George McGinnis, Moses Malone, Spencer Haywood, Louis Dampier, Bob Netolicky, Mack Calvin, Larry Brown, and Freddie Lewis. These players, and their respective teammates in the ABA, forged a cultural and athletic bridge between the “old school” NBA of the 50’s and 60’s and the NBA we know today. The ABA’s fingerprints are all over the way the game of basketball is played today.

Legacy
